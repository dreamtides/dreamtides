# Domain 1: Rejection & Passing Mechanisms

## Key Takeaways

- **Rejection-based algorithms use the richest input signal in the draft.** The player passes 3 cards per pick but only takes 1 -- rejection mechanisms extract 3x more information per pick than selection-based systems, creating a naturally noisy, high-bandwidth signal that resists degenerating into mechanical predictability.
- **Temporary removal is the strongest single mechanism.** Removing passed cards from the pool for a fixed number of picks is simple, produces natural variance (the exact cards removed differ each time), and converges because a Warriors player repeatedly passing off-archetype cards thins them from the pool.
- **Cooldown-based removal naturally solves the early-open/late-convergent tension.** Early in the draft, few cards are on cooldown so packs are diverse. Late in the draft, accumulated cooldowns thin off-archetype cards, concentrating the pool -- no thresholds or state transitions needed.
- **Pure rejection mechanisms converge slowly at archetype level.** Because each resonance is shared by 4 archetypes, removing "Tide cards you passed on" removes both good and bad Tide cards for a Warriors player. Algorithms need to remove enough cards (or for long enough durations) to meaningfully shift archetype-level density.
- **Rejection mechanisms reward signal reading intrinsically.** If many Ember cards keep appearing despite other players' (or the algorithm's) removal pressure, Ember is likely over-represented -- a natural signal that emerges from the mechanism itself rather than being layered on as an afterthought.
- **The biggest risk is over-thinning.** Aggressive removal can shrink the effective pool so much that packs become repetitive (seeing the same cards) or the algorithm runs out of valid candidates for a resonance. All proposals need a pool-size floor or a maximum simultaneous cooldown count.
- **Hybrid rejection (combining removal with return-boosting) outperforms pure removal.** When passed cards return after cooldown, adding a small bias toward cards that share resonance with drafted cards creates a "current" effect -- off-archetype cards cycle out and on-archetype cards cycle back in faster.

## Proposal 1: Cooldown Exile

**One-sentence description:** Each card you pass on is removed from the draft pool for your next 5 picks, then returns.

**Technical description:** When the player sees a 4-card pack and picks 1, the 3 unpicked cards are placed in a cooldown queue tagged with a counter of 5. At the start of each subsequent pick, all cooldown counters decrement by 1; cards reaching 0 return to the pool. Packs are generated by drawing 4 random cards from the active pool (cards not on cooldown). No weighting, no slot assignment -- pure uniform random from a shrinking-then-growing pool.

**Design goal assessment:**
- Strong: Simplicity (one sentence, fully specified), natural variance (pool composition shifts unpredictably), open-ended early (no cooldowns accumulated yet), signal reading (over-represented resonances persist in packs despite removal pressure).
- Weak: Convergence may be too slow at archetype level. Removing 3 cards for 5 picks means ~15 cards are on cooldown at any time -- only ~4% of the 360-card pool. The archetype-level density shift is marginal. Flexible archetypes are supported but there is no active mechanism to help bridge decks.

**Best symbol distribution:** Mostly 2-symbol cards (55%). More symbols per card means each passed card carries more resonance information, but the algorithm does not use symbol counts -- it just removes cards. Distribution matters less here than for accumulation-based systems.

## Proposal 2: Rejection Cascade

**One-sentence description:** Each card you pass on is removed from the pool, and one additional random card sharing its primary resonance is also removed for 4 picks.

**Technical description:** When the player passes on a card, that card enters cooldown (4 picks). Additionally, one random card from the active pool that shares the passed card's primary resonance is also placed on cooldown (4 picks). This creates a cascade: passing on a Tide card removes that card plus one more Tide card. A player committed to Warriors who passes on 3 off-archetype cards (say Ember, Stone, Ember-primary) removes those 3 plus 3 more cards from those resonances -- 6 total removals per pick, meaningfully thinning off-archetype density.

**Design goal assessment:**
- Strong: Convergence (6 removals/pick = ~24 cards on cooldown, a meaningful 7% pool reduction concentrated in off-archetype resonances), natural variance (the cascade target is random -- sometimes it hits a great card, sometimes junk), not on rails (no permanent commitment).
- Weak: Slightly complex (the cascade mechanic adds a clause to the one-sentence description), could over-thin resonances if the player consistently passes the same type. Risk of removing on-archetype cards via cascade (a passed [Tide, Stone] card cascades into another Tide card, which might be a Warriors card the player wanted).

**Best symbol distribution:** Heavy 1-symbol cards (40%) so that cascade removal is more targeted -- a 1-symbol Ember card cascades into another Ember card, cleanly removing Ember density without collateral resonance damage.

## Proposal 3: Rejection Memory with Weighted Return

**One-sentence description:** Cards you pass on are removed for 3 picks; when they return, cards sharing your most-drafted resonance return first, and cards sharing your least-drafted resonance return last.

**Technical description:** Passed cards enter cooldown (base 3 picks). When a card's cooldown expires, its return priority is determined by overlap with the player's resonance symbol counts: cards whose primary resonance matches the player's highest-count resonance return immediately, while cards whose primary resonance matches the player's lowest-count resonance have their cooldown extended by 2 additional picks. This creates a "current" -- on-archetype cards cycle through cooldown quickly, off-archetype cards stay away longer, and the active pool gradually skews toward the player's archetype.

**Design goal assessment:**
- Strong: Convergence (differential return times concentrate the pool), natural variance (return timing is stochastic because it depends on which specific cards were passed and when), flexible archetypes (the system tracks resonance counts, not archetypes, so bridge decks naturally work).
- Weak: Complexity -- the return-priority rule adds meaningful cognitive load beyond "removed for N picks." Signal reading is weakened because the pool is being actively shaped, masking the starting distribution. Two interacting systems (cooldown + return priority) may be hard to explain in one sentence.

**Best symbol distribution:** 2-symbol dominant (55%). The return-priority check uses primary resonance, and 2-symbol cards give a clear primary resonance signal for sorting.

## Proposal 4: Exile Pressure

**One-sentence description:** Each resonance has an exile counter starting at 0; when you pass a card, its primary resonance's counter increases by 1; when generating a pack, each card is independently skipped with probability equal to (its primary resonance's exile counter / 30), rerolling from the full pool on a skip.

**Technical description:** Maintain 4 exile counters (one per resonance), all starting at 0. When the player passes a card, increment the counter for that card's primary resonance by 1 (generic cards increment nothing). When generating a pack, for each of the 4 slots: draw a random card from the full pool, then perform a rejection-sampling check -- if the card's primary resonance has an exile counter of E, reject it with probability E/30 and redraw. This means a resonance with exile counter 15 has a 50% chance of being skipped on each draw. Counters decay by 1 per pick (minimum 0) to prevent runaway exile.

**Design goal assessment:**
- Strong: Natural variance (rejection sampling is inherently stochastic -- the same exile pressure sometimes blocks a card, sometimes doesn't), convergence (by pick 10, a committed player has passed ~15 cards from off-archetype resonances, building exile pressure of 5-8 against those resonances, giving ~20-25% skip chance), not on rails (counters decay, so pivoting is possible -- old exile pressure fades over ~8 picks).
- Weak: The probability math is invisible to the player -- they cannot predict or observe the exile counters directly. Convergence may be insufficient at archetype level because exile pressure operates on resonance, not archetype. Slightly more complex than pure cooldown (counter + decay + probability).

**Best symbol distribution:** Mostly 1-symbol (40%) and 2-symbol (45%). 1-symbol cards generate clean exile pressure against a single resonance; 2-symbol cards provide the archetype differentiation needed for evaluation.

## Proposal 5: Passed-Card Graveyard with Resonance Tides

**One-sentence description:** Cards you pass accumulate in a discard graveyard; every 5 picks, the resonance with the most cards in the graveyard has 10 of its cards removed from the pool until end of draft, and the graveyard empties.

**Technical description:** All passed cards go into a graveyard (no cooldown -- they remain in the active pool). Every 5 picks, the algorithm counts the primary resonance of all cards in the graveyard, identifies the resonance with the highest count, removes 10 random cards of that resonance from the active pool permanently, then empties the graveyard. This creates discrete "tide" events that reshape the pool in waves. A committed Warriors player who consistently passes Ember and Stone cards will see those resonances thinned in waves at picks 5, 10, 15, 20, 25.

**Design goal assessment:**
- Strong: Creates dramatic pool-shaping moments that the player can anticipate and plan around. Signal reading is excellent -- the tide events are observable (sudden absence of a resonance). Natural variance between tides (packs are fully random between wave events). Simple core loop.
- Weak: The batch processing feels mechanical -- "every 5 picks" is an arbitrary cadence. Permanent removal risks over-thinning by endgame (5 waves x 10 cards = 50 cards permanently removed). A player who passes on a mix of resonances gets diffuse, weak tides. Early packs (picks 1-5) have zero convergence pressure.

**Best symbol distribution:** Heavy 2-symbol (60%). The algorithm only counts primary resonance for tide determination, but 2-symbol cards ensure enough resonance diversity in the pool that thinning one resonance does not accidentally destroy another.

## Champion Selection: Exile Pressure (Proposal 4)

**Why Exile Pressure wins:**

Exile Pressure is the strongest balance of simplicity, natural variance, and convergence among the five proposals. Here is the reasoning:

- **Cooldown Exile** (P1) is the simplest but converges too slowly -- 4% pool reduction is not enough to shift archetype-level density meaningfully.
- **Rejection Cascade** (P2) converges faster but the cascade mechanic risks removing on-archetype cards and adds complexity.
- **Rejection Memory** (P3) has the best convergence story but violates the simplicity test -- two interacting systems (cooldown + return priority) cannot fit in one honest sentence.
- **Resonance Tides** (P5) creates dramatic moments but the batch cadence is arbitrary and early picks have zero convergence pressure.
- **Exile Pressure** (P4) operates on every pick, uses rejection sampling (a well-understood probabilistic primitive), decays naturally to allow pivoting, and produces inherently stochastic pack composition. The one-sentence description fully specifies the algorithm. The exile counters are invisible to the player, but the *effect* is visible -- "I keep passing Ember cards and I'm seeing fewer of them" is a natural observation.

The critical advantage: Exile Pressure turns rejection into a continuous, decaying probabilistic bias rather than a discrete removal. This means the pool never actually shrinks (all cards remain available), avoiding the over-thinning and card-repetition problems of removal-based approaches. The probability-based filtering creates natural variance on every single pack draw.

## Champion Deep-Dive: Exile Pressure

### Example Draft Sequences

**Early committer (commits to Warriors by pick 5):**
- Picks 1-3: Sees diverse packs (all exile counters near 0). Picks a Tide/Zephyr card, then another Tide card, then a Zephyr/Tide card. Passes ~9 cards total, roughly 3-4 Ember-primary, 2-3 Stone-primary, 1-2 Tide/Zephyr cards they did not want.
- By pick 4: Ember exile counter ~4, Stone exile counter ~3. Each Ember card drawn has a 13% chance of being rerolled. Slight bias beginning.
- Picks 5-8: Exile counters grow. Ember hits ~8 (27% skip), Stone hits ~6 (20% skip). But counters also decay by 1/pick, so net growth slows. Packs noticeably shift: fewer Ember/Stone cards, more Tide/Zephyr. But not reliably -- some packs still show 2 Ember cards that survived the rejection check.
- Picks 15-20: Exile counters reach equilibrium around 8-12 for off-archetype resonances (new passes roughly equal decay). Skip probability stabilizes around 30-40%. The player sees ~2 archetype-relevant cards per pack on average, but with real variance: some packs have 3, some have 1, occasionally 0.

**Flexible player (stays open through pick 10):**
- Picks 1-10: Drafts the best card regardless of resonance. Passes are spread across all resonances. Exile counters build evenly -- maybe 3-5 for each resonance. Skip probabilities are low and uniform (~10-15% per resonance). Packs remain nearly random, which is correct: the system is not forcing commitment.
- Pick 10+: Begins focusing on one archetype. Off-archetype exile counters start climbing differentially while on-archetype counters decay. Convergence is slower than the early committer (reaching equilibrium around pick 18 instead of 12) but still occurs.

**Pivot attempt (commits Warriors picks 1-7, pivots to Storm picks 8+):**
- Picks 1-7: Builds Ember/Stone exile pressure while taking Tide/Zephyr cards. Ember counter ~8, Stone counter ~6.
- Pick 8: Decides to pivot to Storm (Ember/Stone). Starts taking Ember cards and passing Tide/Zephyr.
- Picks 8-12: Old Ember/Stone exile pressure decays (losing ~1/pick). New Tide/Zephyr exile pressure builds. By pick 12, the exile landscape has substantially shifted: Ember down to ~4, Tide up to ~6. The pivot is not instant but it is real -- the decay mechanic means old pressure fades in ~8 picks.
- Picks 13+: Exile pressure now reflects the new archetype. Pivot complete but at the cost of ~5 picks of suboptimal convergence.

### Predicted Failure Modes

1. **Archetype-level convergence may be insufficient.** Exile pressure operates on resonance, not archetype. Suppressing Ember reduces both Storm and Blink cards. For a Warriors player, suppressing Ember and Stone removes cards from 4 archetypes (Storm, Blink, Self-Discard, Self-Mill) -- but the remaining Tide and Zephyr cards are split across 4 archetypes too (Warriors, Sacrifice, Ramp, Flash). Only ~50% of surviving Tide cards are S/A for Warriors. This means the archetype-level convergence is roughly half the resonance-level convergence. If equilibrium skip probability is 35% for off-archetype resonances, the effective archetype density increase might only be ~15-20%, giving perhaps 1.7-1.9 S/A cards per pack instead of the target 2.0.

2. **Power-chasing players get almost no convergence.** A player who always picks the highest-power card regardless of resonance will pass a mix of resonances, building diffuse exile pressure. The system rewards archetype commitment and penalizes pure power-chasing, which is arguably correct behavior but may frustrate some players.

3. **Invisible state.** Players cannot directly see exile counters. The effect manifests as "I see fewer Ember cards" but the quantitative mechanism is hidden. This may feel like the draft is "doing something" without the player understanding what -- less transparent than Lane Locking's visible slot locks.

### Parameter Variants Worth Testing

1. **Exile increment / decay / divisor:** The base proposal uses +1 per passed card (primary resonance), -1 decay per pick, and /30 for probability. Testing +1/-1/20 (faster convergence, max skip 50% at counter 10), +1/-0.5/30 (slower decay, stronger memory), and +2/-1/30 (faster buildup from multi-symbol passes) would map the sensitivity surface.

2. **Multi-symbol exile:** Instead of only incrementing the primary resonance counter, increment all resonance counters matching the passed card's symbols (primary +2, secondary +1). This doubles the exile pressure input but spreads it across resonances. May improve convergence for 2-resonance archetypes.

3. **Exile floor/ceiling:** Cap exile counters at 15 (50% skip at /30) to prevent extreme thinning late in the draft. Add a floor of 0 (already specified) and test whether a minimum exile of 1 for all resonances after pick 10 helps convergence without hurting early openness.

### Proposed Symbol Distribution for Simulation

| Symbol Count | % of Non-Generic | Cards |
|---|---|---|
| 0 (generic) | -- | 36 |
| 1 symbol | 35% | 113 |
| 2 symbols | 50% | 162 |
| 3 symbols | 15% | 49 |

Rationale: A 35/50/15 split gives most cards a clear primary resonance for exile targeting while 2-symbol cards provide the secondary resonance needed for archetype differentiation. The 15% 3-symbol cards create occasional high-signal picks (a 3-symbol card generates 4 weighted symbols when drafted, or 1 exile increment when passed) without dominating the pool.
